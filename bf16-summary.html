Executive Summary

We re-ran the complete GRAFT pipeline using bfloat16 (full precision) models instead of 4-bit quantized ones. bf16 eliminates quantization noise and gives a cleaner measurement of how well the proxy-tuning delta transfers across model sizes.

Key headline: Delta-steered 14B base achieves 57.1% IFEval strict accuracy on all 541 prompts — up from 40.7% for the raw 14B base, closing 78% of the gap to the 8B instruct ceiling (61.6%). With zero fine-tuning of the 14B model.

What bf16 buys you

  • Better distributional fidelity: the recovered distribution is 29% closer to the instruct model (JS divergence)
  • Higher top-1 agreement: 89.5% vs 85.8% across 541 prompts
  • Lower noise floor: ~0.03 nats vs ~0.1 nats, making metrics more trustworthy
  • Stronger delta signal: mean |$\delta$| ~28 vs ~24, preserving more fine-tuning information
  • Cost: ~4x more memory (62 GB peak vs ~18 GB for 4-bit), ~2x slower inference

8. Conclusions

Running GRAFT with bf16 models confirms and strengthens the 4-bit findings:

  1. Formula is correct (Step 1): Full-vocab KL = 0 exactly in oracle mode. bf16 reduces restricted KL precision noise by 5.5x.
  2. Delta carries stronger signal at bf16 (Step 2): Mean |$\delta$| is 17% larger, meaning quantization compresses the fine-tuning information.
  3. Cross-model transfer improves (Step 3): JS(ft, recovered) drops from 0.105 to 0.016 — an 85% reduction in distributional distance.
  4. At scale (541 IFEval): 89.5% top-1 match (was 85.8%), 95.7% at k=10 (was 92.1%).
  5. Full generation: 57.1% IFEval accuracy vs 40.7% base — a 16.4pp improvement with zero fine-tuning, closing 78% of the gap to the instruct ceiling.
  6. Delta beats instruct on some categories: change_case (92% vs 85%), combination (91% vs 89%), startend (63% vs 61%) — the 14B base's capacity enhances the delta signal.

The proxy tuning formula:

$$p_{\text{steered}} = \text{softmax}\bigl(s_{\text{large}} + \lambda \cdot (\log p_{\text{ft}}^{S_t} - \log p_{\text{base}}^{S_t})\bigr)$$

works better with bf16 because the delta $(\log p_{\text{ft}} - \log p_{\text{base}})$ is computed with higher fidelity, and the large model's logit landscape is more nuanced. The tradeoff is memory and speed, but for applications where quality matters more than latency, bf16 is the clear winner.
