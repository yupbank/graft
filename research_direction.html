Research Direction: Probability-Weighted Reward Concentration

The one theoretical finding from GRAFT that could be a paper

1. The Puzzle

We measured the behavioral delta between an instruct model and its base across 151,936 tokens. Two facts that seem contradictory:

Fact 1: The delta is everywhere.

99.9% of tokens have $|\delta(y)| > 1$. The delta entropy is 11.91 out of max 11.93. Instruction-tuning changes the probability of virtually every token in the vocabulary.

Fact 2: Restricting to top-50 tokens recovers 87% of the effect.

GRAFT uses only the top-50 tokens from the large model (0.03% of vocabulary) and achieves 59.7% vs proxy-tuning's 62.5% on IFEval. It discards 99.97% of the delta mass but keeps most of the functional impact.

How can 0.03% of the vocabulary carry 87% of the effect if the delta is uniformly distributed?

2. The Key Insight: Probability Weighting

The raw delta $|\delta(y)|$ is uniform. But generation doesn't treat all tokens equally — it only cares about tokens the model actually considers generating. The probability-weighted delta is concentrated:

$$\text{Raw (unweighted):} \quad \sum_{y \in V} |\delta(y)| \approx \text{spread across all 151,936 tokens}$$
$$\text{Functional (probability-weighted):} \quad \sum_{y \in V} p_{\text{large}}(y) \cdot |\delta(y)| \approx \text{concentrated in top-50}$$

The large model assigns $p > 0.01$ to maybe 50 tokens. All other tokens have $p \approx 0$. Even though $|\delta(y)|$ is large for those tokens, $p_{\text{large}}(y) \cdot |\delta(y)| \approx 0$ because $p$ is near zero. The delta on token #10,000 doesn't matter because that token was never going to be generated anyway.

3. Formalization

3.1 The Reward Framework

Any fine-tuned model implicitly optimizes a reward (Korbak et al., 2022):

$$r(y \mid x) = \beta \log \frac{p_{\text{instruct}}(y \mid x)}{p_{\text{base}}(y \mid x)}$$

Proxy-tuning transfers this reward from a small pair to a large model:

$$p_{\text{steered}}(y \mid x) \propto p_{\text{large}}(y \mid x) \cdot \exp\!\left(\frac{1}{\beta} \hat{r}(y \mid x)\right)$$

where $\hat{r}(y \mid x) = \beta \log \frac{p_{\text{small-inst}}(y \mid x)}{p_{\text{small-base}}(y \mid x)}$ is the proxy reward.

3.2 The Concentration Conjecture

Conjecture: For models from the same family, the probability-weighted reward is concentrated in the top-$k$:

$$\frac{\sum_{y \in \text{top-}k(p_{\text{large}})} p_{\text{large}}(y) \cdot |\hat{r}(y)|}{\sum_{y \in V} p_{\text{large}}(y) \cdot |\hat{r}(y)|} \;\geq\; 1 - \epsilon(k)$$

where $\epsilon(k)$ decays rapidly. Our empirics suggest $\epsilon(50) \leq 0.13$ (87% recovery).

3.3 Why This Might Be Provable

The key property: $p_{\text{large}}(y)$ is sharply peaked. For a typical next-token distribution:

  • Top-1 token: $p \approx 0.5\text{-}0.9$
  • Top-10 tokens: cumulative $p \approx 0.95$
  • Top-50 tokens: cumulative $p \approx 0.99$
  • Remaining 151,886 tokens: cumulative $p \approx 0.01$

So even if $|\hat{r}(y)|$ is the same for all tokens, the probability weighting ensures 99% of the functional reward mass sits in the top-50. The "tail" of 151,886 tokens contributes at most 1% of the weighted reward, regardless of how large their individual $|\hat{r}|$ values are.

$$\sum_{y \notin \text{top-}k} p_{\text{large}}(y) \cdot |\hat{r}(y)| \;\leq\; \underbrace{\left(\sum_{y \notin \text{top-}k} p_{\text{large}}(y)\right)}_{\leq\, 1 - \sum_{\text{top-}k} p_{\text{large}}} \cdot \max_{y} |\hat{r}(y)|$$

If $\sum_{\text{top-}k} p_{\text{large}} \geq 1 - \alpha$ (the top-$k$ covers $1-\alpha$ of the probability mass), then the tail reward is bounded by $\alpha \cdot \|\hat{r}\|_\infty$. For $k=50$ and typical LLM distributions, $\alpha \leq 0.01$.

4. What This Explains

Empirical FindingExplanation
GRAFT k=50 closes 87% of the gap Top-50 captures $\geq 99\%$ of probability mass, hence $\geq 87\%$ of probability-weighted reward
Proxy-tuning (full vocab) is 3pp better The remaining 1% probability mass in the tail still contributes some reward — small but nonzero
Raw delta looks full-rank Unweighted $|\delta|$ is uniform — correct, but irrelevant to generation
Top-k boost (uniform constant) doesn't work Uniform boost $\neq$ reward. The reward has direction (which tokens up, which down). A constant doesn't carry directional information.
Instruct models are immune to steering Instruct models have even more peaked distributions ($p_{\text{top-1}} \approx 1.0$). The "consideration set" is 1 token, not 50. No room for the delta to operate.
GRAFT wins on change_case but loses on length Case decisions are local (one token matters) — top-k captures it. Length planning requires coordination across many tokens — the probability-weighted reward at any single step doesn't capture the multi-step constraint.

5. What's Needed for a Paper

5.1 Theory

  1. Formalize the probability-weighted reward concentration as a theorem
  2. Prove a bound: $\epsilon(k) \leq f(\alpha, \|\hat{r}\|_\infty)$ where $\alpha$ = tail probability mass
  3. Show this bound is tight (construct a case where the bound is achieved)
  4. Derive the optimal $k^*$ as a function of the distribution's entropy

5.2 Experiments

  1. Multiple model families: Qwen3, Llama3, Gemma — does the concentration hold universally?
  2. Measure the bound empirically: plot $\epsilon(k)$ vs $k$ across 541 prompts, show it matches the theoretical bound
  3. Predict GRAFT/proxy gap: use the bound to predict the gap for each prompt, verify correlation
  4. Different tasks: code generation, math reasoning — does the bound change?

5.3 Story

Paper title idea: "Why Behavioral Transfer Works with 0.03% of the Vocabulary: Probability-Weighted Reward Concentration in Language Model Steering"

One-paragraph pitch: Proxy-tuning steers large language models at inference time by adding a behavioral delta from a smaller model pair. The delta is full-rank across the vocabulary, yet restricting it to just 50 tokens (0.03%) recovers 87% of the effect. We explain this via probability-weighted reward concentration: while the raw reward is uniform, the probability-weighted reward — the only part that affects generation — is sharply concentrated in the model's top-$k$ tokens. We prove a bound showing the concentration follows directly from the peaked nature of LLM output distributions, and verify it across three model families and two benchmarks.
