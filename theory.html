GRAFT — Theoretical Framework

Connecting proxy-tuning, restricted deltas, and the reward transfer hypothesis

1. The Reward Interpretation of Fine-Tuning

The starting point is the KL-penalized RL objective (Korbak et al., 2022). Given a pretrained model $\mathcal{M}$:

$$\arg\max_{\mathcal{M}^*} \;\; \mathbb{E}_{y \sim p_{\mathcal{M}}(\cdot|x)}\bigl[r(x,y)\bigr] - \beta \,\text{KL}\bigl(p_{\mathcal{M}}(\cdot|x) \;\|\; p_{\mathcal{M}^*}(\cdot|x)\bigr) \qquad (1)$$

This has a closed-form solution:

$$p_{\mathcal{M}^*}(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z}\, p_{\mathcal{M}}(y \mid x) \,\exp\!\left(\frac{1}{\beta}\,r(x,y)\right) \qquad (2)$$

Rearranging, any fine-tuned model $\mathcal{M}^*$ can be viewed as implicitly optimizing a reward:

$$r(x,y) = \beta \log \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^*}(y \mid x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}}(y \mid x)} \qquad (3)$$

Key insight: The reward is simply the log-probability ratio between the fine-tuned and base models, scaled by $\beta$. This is exactly what proxy-tuning and GRAFT compute as the "delta."

2. Proxy-Tuning as Reward Transfer

Proxy-tuning (Liu et al., 2024) approximates the large model's fine-tuning by using a small model pair:

$$p_{\mathcal{M}^*}(y \mid x) \;\propto\; p_{\mathcal{M}}(y \mid x) \cdot \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^+}(y \mid x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}^-}(y \mid x)} \qquad (4)$$

where $\mathcal{M}^+$ = small tuned (expert), $\mathcal{M}^-$ = small untuned (anti-expert), $\mathcal{M}$ = large base model.

In the reward framework, this means proxy-tuning applies the small model's implicit reward to the large model:

$$\hat{r}_{\text{proxy}}(x,y) = \beta \log \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^+}(y \mid x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}^-}(y \mid x)}$$

The transfer assumption: Proxy-tuning works to the extent that the small pair's implicit reward $\hat{r}_{\text{proxy}}$ equals the reward that would result from directly fine-tuning the large model.

In other words: instruction-following is a reward that transfers across model sizes.

3. What GRAFT Changes: Restricted Reward

GRAFT restricts the reward computation to a candidate set $S_t = \text{top-}k(s_{\text{large}})$:

$$\hat{r}_{\text{GRAFT}}(x,y) = \beta \log \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^+}^{S_t}(y \mid x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}^-}^{S_t}(y \mid x)} \qquad \text{for } y \in S_t$$

where $p^{S_t}$ denotes probability renormalized over $S_t$ only:

$$p_{\mathcal{M}^+}^{S_t}(y \mid x) = \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^+}(y \mid x)}{\sum_{y' \in S_t} p_{\mathcal{M}^+}(y' \mid x)}$$

This is a conditional reward — the reward for choosing token $y$ given that we're already restricting to the large model's top candidates.

4. Why Restricted Reward Can Be Better

The reward transfer assumption holds unevenly across the vocabulary. Define the reward transfer gap:

$$\varepsilon(y) = \left| r_{\text{large}}(x,y) - \hat{r}_{\text{small}}(x,y) \right|$$

4.1 Top-k tokens: $\varepsilon$ is small

For tokens the large model considers plausible (high $p_{\mathcal{M}}(y|x)$), both the small and large models have assigned meaningful probability through training. Their rewards reflect real learned preferences. The transfer gap is small.

4.2 Tail tokens: $\varepsilon$ is large

For tokens ranked #1000+ (near-zero $p_{\mathcal{M}}(y|x)$), the large model's probability is dominated by logit-head weight artifacts, not learned behavior. The small model's reward for these tokens is similarly meaningless. The transfer gap is large and unpredictable.

GRAFT minimizes the expected transfer gap by restricting to $S_t$ where $\varepsilon$ is small:

$$\mathbb{E}_{y \in S_t}\bigl[\varepsilon(y)\bigr] \;\ll\; \mathbb{E}_{y \in V}\bigl[\varepsilon(y)\bigr]$$

Full proxy-tuning averages over both regimes. GRAFT restricts to the regime where the reward transfer assumption actually holds.

5. The Three Methods Compared

MethodFormulaReward space
Direct fine-tuning $p_{\mathcal{M}^*}(y|x) \propto p_{\mathcal{M}}(y|x) \exp\!\bigl(\tfrac{1}{\beta} r(x,y)\bigr)$ True reward (requires training)
Proxy-tuning $p(y|x) \propto p_{\mathcal{M}}(y|x) \cdot \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^+}(y|x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}^-}(y|x)}$ Full-vocab transferred reward
GRAFT $p(y|x) \propto p_{\mathcal{M}}(y|x) \cdot \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^+}^{S_t}(y|x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}^-}^{S_t}(y|x)}$ for $y \in S_t$ Restricted transferred reward

All three are the same formula — the only difference is what vocabulary the reward is computed over.

7. Why Restriction Helps at Token 1 but May Hurt at Token $t$

At step 1: $S_t = \text{top-}k(s_{\text{large}})$ is computed from the large model's distribution over the prompt. The candidate set is fresh and well-calibrated. Restricting to it filters noise effectively.

At step $t > 1$: the prefix was generated by the delta-steered model, not by the large model alone. The large model's top-$k$ is now conditioned on a prefix it didn't generate, which may cause:

  1. Candidate drift: The large model's top-$k$ may not contain the token that would be best under the delta-steered distribution. Good tokens get excluded.
  2. Error compounding: A suboptimal token at step $t$ shifts the prefix, changing $S_{t+1}$, potentially cascading into worse candidates.
  3. Fork starvation: At "fork" positions (multiple valid continuations), $k=50$ may be too restrictive. Proxy-tuning's full vocab preserves all options.

The theory predicts: GRAFT should benefit from larger $k$ during generation than during single-token analysis. The optimal $k$ is a function of:

$$k^* = \arg\min_k \;\; \underbrace{\text{noise from tail tokens}}_{\text{decreases with smaller } k} + \underbrace{\text{loss from excluded good tokens}}_{\text{increases with smaller } k}$$

At step 1, the noise term dominates ($k^*$ is small). At step $t \gg 1$, the exclusion term grows ($k^*$ should be larger).

8. Paths to Improvement

8.1 Adaptive $k$ per generation step

$$k_t = k_0 + \alpha \cdot t$$

Start with aggressive restriction ($k_0 = 10$) and widen as generation proceeds. Or use the entropy of the large model's distribution: low entropy (confident) → small $k$, high entropy (uncertain) → large $k$.

8.2 Soft weighting instead of hard restriction

$$\text{score}(i) = \log p_{\mathcal{M}}(i) + \lambda \cdot w(i) \cdot \delta(i)$$

where $w(i) = \sigma\bigl(\log p_{\mathcal{M}}(i) - \tau\bigr)$ is a sigmoid gate that smoothly downweights the delta for low-probability tokens. This interpolates between GRAFT ($w = \mathbb{1}_{S_t}$) and proxy-tuning ($w = 1$).

$$\lambda_t = \frac{\lambda_0}{1 + \gamma \cdot H(p_{\mathcal{M}}(\cdot | x_{

At high-entropy (fork) positions, reduce $\lambda$ to let the large model's own preferences dominate. At low-entropy (lock) positions, full $\lambda$ since the delta is confident.

8.4 SSD-enhanced delta

Use Apple's self-distillation to sharpen the small model before computing the delta. This reduces the effective rank of the delta, making restriction more effective:

$$\hat{r}_{\text{SSD+GRAFT}} = \beta \log \frac{p_{\text{SSD}(\mathcal{M}^+)}^{S_t}(y|x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}^-}^{S_t}(y|x)}$$

9. The Unifying Theory

Behavioral specialization is low-rank in reward space

Fine-tuning changes the output distribution by applying a reward $r(x,y) = \beta \log \frac{p_{\mathcal{M}^*}(y|x)}{p_{\mathcal{M}}(y|x)}$. Our experiments show this reward has effective rank ~10 in the token space: only ~10 tokens per position have meaningfully non-zero reward.

This explains:

Testable prediction: The effective rank of the reward (measurable as the number of tokens with $|\delta(i)| > \tau$ at each position) should predict which capabilities transfer well via GRAFT and which require full fine-tuning.

11. The Top-$k$ Boost Experiment: What Doesn't Work

If SSD = self-distillation on top-$k$ filtered outputs, and fine-tuning $\approx$ logit update, can we just add a constant to the top-$k$ logits?

$$\text{score}(i) = s_{\text{large}}(i) + \alpha \cdot \mathbb{1}[i \in \text{top-}k(s_{\text{large}})]$$
ConfigPrompt StrictInstruction
14B base (no boost)40.0%48.7%
$k=10$, $\alpha=5$42.0%50.0%
$k=50$, $\alpha=5$42.0%50.0%
$k=200$, $\alpha=5$42.0%50.0%

Result: Uniform boost barely helps (+2pp). Compare with GRAFT (+18pp) and proxy-tuning (+21pp) on the same 50 prompts.

$k$ doesn't matter — $k=10$, $k=50$, $k=200$ give identical results at each $\alpha$. A constant boost doesn't know which tokens to promote. Both Here (good for instruction-following) and import (bad) get the same boost when they're both in the top-$k$.

This proves that SSD's value is NOT uniform support compression. SSD learns token-specific reward shaping — promoting some tokens and demoting others within the top-$k$. That's exactly what the delta $\log p_{\text{ft}}^{S_t} - \log p_{\text{raw}}^{S_t}$ provides: a directional signal, not a uniform boost.

12. The Complete Picture: What We Learned

Takeaway 1: The delta is the key ingredient

Without a fine-tuned model to provide direction, you can't steer a base model. A uniform logit boost (+2pp) is negligible compared to proxy-tuning (+21pp) or GRAFT (+18pp). You need to know which tokens to promote and which to demote — that's what instruction-tuning learns, and what the delta transfers.

Takeaway 2: Full-vocab delta beats restricted delta for generation

First-token (distribution matching)Multi-token (generation)
GRAFT $k=10$92.1% top-1 match (best)not tested
GRAFT $k=50$88.4%59.7%
Proxy-tuning (full vocab)85.8%62.5% (best)

Restriction helps for single-token distribution matching (filters tail noise). But for multi-token generation, the full vocab preserves options needed at "fork" positions. The gap comes almost entirely from length_constraints (proxy 52% vs GRAFT 38%).

Takeaway 3: GRAFT and proxy-tuning have complementary strengths

Takeaway 4: The reward is directional, not uniform

The top-$k$ boost experiment disproves the hypothesis that "just sharpen the distribution" is enough. The behavioral reward has direction — specific tokens go up, specific tokens go down. This direction transfers across model sizes (8B $\to$ 14B) and is concentrated in ~10 tokens per position, but it must be the right 10 tokens.

Takeaway 5: Open question — can we get the best of both?

The ideal method would use:

  • Full vocabulary (like proxy-tuning) for coverage at fork positions
  • Noise filtering (like GRAFT) for precision at lock positions
  • An automatic way to detect lock vs fork (perhaps via entropy of $p_{\text{large}}$)

Candidate approach: entropy-adaptive restriction

$$k_t = \begin{cases} 10 & \text{if } H(p_{\text{large}}) < \tau \quad \text{(lock: model is confident)} \\ |V| & \text{if } H(p_{\text{large}}) \geq \tau \quad \text{(fork: model is uncertain)} \end{cases}$$

This would apply GRAFT's restriction where it helps (low-entropy/precision tokens) and fall back to proxy-tuning where restriction hurts (high-entropy/planning tokens).
